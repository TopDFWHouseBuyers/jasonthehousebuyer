Understanding Texas Community Property Laws

Texas is one of nine community property states, which means that most assets acquired during your marriage—including your home—are considered jointly owned by both spouses. This applies whether the house is in Sugar Land, Pearland, or any other Houston suburb.

However, there are important exceptions:

  • Property owned before the marriage remains separate property
  • Gifts or inheritances received by one spouse stay separate
  • Property purchased with separate funds may not be community property
  • The way the title is held doesn't necessarily determine ownership in a divorce

Even if only one spouse's name is on the mortgage or deed, the house may still be considered community property if it was purchased during the marriage. This is why many Houston divorcing couples need legal guidance to determine actual ownership stakes.

Your Options for the Family Home During Divorce

When it comes to your Houston property, you typically have three main options:

One Spouse Buys Out the Other

This works when one person wants to keep the house and can afford to refinance the mortgage in their name alone. They'll need to compensate the other spouse for their equity share. In Houston's current market, this can be substantial, especially in areas like The Woodlands or Memorial where home values have appreciated significantly.

The challenge? Qualifying for a new mortgage on a single income while going through divorce can be difficult. Lenders will scrutinize your debt-to-income ratio, and divorce-related expenses often make this option unfeasible.

Continue Co-Owning the Property

Some couples choose to keep the house temporarily, especially if they want to wait until children graduate or until the Houston real estate market improves. However, this requires ongoing cooperation and shared financial responsibility—something that can be complicated after a divorce.

You'll both remain liable for the mortgage, property taxes, and maintenance. If one person stops paying, it affects both credit scores. This option works best when the relationship remains amicable.

Sell the House and Split the Proceeds

For many Houston couples, selling the home and dividing the equity is the cleanest solution. It allows both parties to move forward financially independent and start fresh. This is often the fastest way to resolve property division, especially when neither spouse can afford the home alone or when you need funds for separate living arrangements.

When Should You Sell During the Divorce Process?

Timing matters significantly in Texas divorces. You have several options for when to sell:

Before Filing for Divorce: Some couples sell their Houston home before officially filing. This can simplify the divorce proceedings since there's one less asset to divide. However, you'll need to agree on how to split proceeds and where that money goes until the divorce is final.

During the Divorce: Most couples sell while the divorce is pending. The proceeds typically go into an escrow account until the judge issues the final decree. This protects both parties and ensures fair distribution.

After the Divorce is Final: The divorce decree will specify terms for selling the property. This gives you certainty but delays your ability to move forward financially.

In Harris County, divorce proceedings can take anywhere from 60 days (the minimum waiting period in Texas) to well over a year for contested divorces. The sooner you address the house, the sooner you can both move on.

Special Considerations for Houston Homeowners

Flood Damage and Hurricane History

If your Houston home experienced flooding during Harvey or any other storm, this needs to be disclosed to potential buyers. Properties in flood zones or with previous flood damage can take longer to sell on the traditional market, which can delay your divorce settlement.

If you need to sell quickly and your home has flood history, a cash sale might be your best option to avoid prolonged listing times.

Current Houston Market Conditions

The Houston real estate market has seen significant fluctuations in recent years. Interest rates, inventory levels, and buyer demand all affect how quickly you can sell and what price you'll receive. In hot neighborhoods like Katy or Sugar Land, homes may sell quickly, but properties needing repairs or in less desirable areas might sit on the market for months.

Consider getting a professional opinion on your home's value and realistic timeline for selling in your specific Houston neighborhood.

Property Taxes and HOA Considerations

Texas property taxes are among the highest in the nation, and Houston homeowners know this well. You'll need to determine how to split property tax payments up until closing. If you're in a community with HOA dues—common in master-planned communities like Bridgeland or Cinco Ranch—those ongoing payments need to be addressed too.

Tax Implications of Selling During Divorce in Texas

While Texas doesn't have state income tax, you still need to consider federal capital gains taxes. The good news: if you've lived in your Houston home as your primary residence for at least two of the last five years, you can exclude up to $250,000 in gains if filing single, or $500,000 if filing jointly.

Timing your sale relative to your divorce finalization can impact your tax situation. If you sell before the divorce is final, you might qualify for the married filing jointly exclusion. Consult with a tax professional familiar with Texas divorce situations to optimize your tax outcome.

You'll also want to consider how the sale proceeds will be reported. Even though Texas is a community property state, the IRS has specific requirements for reporting real estate sales during divorce.

Protecting Your Interests When Selling During Divorce

Even if you're on relatively good terms with your soon-to-be ex-spouse, take these precautions:

Document everything: Keep records of all communications about the house sale, expenses paid, and agreements made.

Use neutral third parties: Having sale proceeds go into an escrow account or trust protects both parties until the divorce is finalized.

Get legal review: Have your attorney review any agreements before signing, especially if you're selling before the divorce is final.

Don't make unilateral decisions: Even if you feel you're entitled to make decisions about the house, acting without agreement can create legal complications and delays.

Consider a partition sale: If you can't agree on anything, Texas law allows for a partition sale where the court orders the property sold and proceeds divided. This is a last resort but is sometimes necessary.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can my spouse force me to sell the house during divorce?

In Texas, if you can't agree on what to do with community property, the court can order a partition sale. This means yes, the house can be sold even if one spouse objects, but typically the judge will consider both parties' situations before making this decision.

What if my spouse won't cooperate with selling?

If your spouse refuses to list the house or sign documents, you may need to ask the court to intervene. Your attorney can file motions to compel cooperation or request the judge issue orders regarding the sale. This delays the process but provides a legal remedy.

Do I need to move out before we can sell?

No, you don't need to move out first. Many Houston couples sell while one or both still live in the home. However, vacant homes sometimes show better. If you're selling for cash to a company like Jason The House Buyer, it doesn't matter whether the home is occupied or vacant.

How are repairs and maintenance costs split before closing?

This should be addressed in your divorce agreement or temporary orders. Often, costs are split proportionally to each spouse's equity share, or one spouse pays and receives credit at closing. When you sell as-is for cash, this issue becomes moot since no repairs are required.

What happens to the mortgage until the house sells?

Both spouses remain legally obligated to the mortgage until it's paid off at closing. Missing payments will damage both credit scores. Your temporary divorce orders should specify who makes payments and how they'll be credited in the final settlement.

Do You Have to Pay Taxes When You Sell Your House in Texas?

Published June 20, 2026 · Houston, Texas
Back to All Articles

If you're planning to sell your home in Houston or anywhere in Texas, you're probably wondering about the tax implications. The good news is that Texas offers some unique advantages for home sellers, but there are still federal taxes to consider.

The Texas Advantage: No State Income Tax

One of the biggest benefits of selling a house in Texas is that the state has no income tax. This means you won't owe any state-level capital gains tax on the profit from your home sale. Whether you're in Houston, Katy, The Woodlands, or Sugar Land, this advantage applies across all of Greater Houston and throughout Texas.

Unlike homeowners in California, New York, or other high-tax states who may pay 5-13% in state taxes on their profits, Texas sellers keep more money in their pockets. This is a significant benefit that many Houston area homeowners don't fully appreciate until they compare their situation to sellers in other states.

Federal Capital Gains Tax: What You Need to Know

While Texas doesn't tax your home sale profits, the federal government still might. The IRS imposes capital gains tax on the profit you make from selling your home, but there's a generous exemption that protects most homeowners.

If you've lived in your home as your primary residence for at least two of the last five years, you can exclude up to $250,000 in profit if you're single, or up to $500,000 if you're married filing jointly. For most Houston homeowners, this exemption means paying zero federal taxes on their home sale.

For example, if you bought your home in Pearland for $200,000 and sell it for $350,000, your profit is $150,000. As a single homeowner who lived there for two years, you'd owe no federal taxes because your gain is under the $250,000 threshold.

When You Might Owe Federal Taxes

There are situations where Houston area sellers might face capital gains taxes. If your profit exceeds the $250,000/$500,000 exemption, you'll pay capital gains tax on the amount over that threshold. The rate depends on your income level, typically ranging from 0% to 20%.

You also might not qualify for the full exemption if you haven't lived in the home for two of the last five years, if you've used the exemption on another property in the past two years, or if the home was an investment property rather than your primary residence.

Investment properties and rental homes in areas like Cypress, Tomball, or League City are treated differently and don't qualify for the primary residence exemption.

Behind on Mortgage Payments in Houston? Here Are Your Options

Published May 16, 2026 · Houston, Texas
Back to All Articles

If you've fallen behind on your mortgage payments in Houston, you're not alone. Whether it's due to job loss, medical bills, divorce, or unexpected expenses, thousands of Harris County homeowners face this stressful situation every year. The good news is that you have several options available, and taking action now can help you avoid foreclosure and protect your financial future.

Understanding the Timeline: How Long Before Foreclosure in Texas?

Texas is known for having one of the fastest foreclosure processes in the country, which makes it especially important to act quickly if you've fallen behind on payments. Under Texas law, your lender can begin the foreclosure process after you're just one payment behind, though most don't start immediately.

Here's the typical timeline for foreclosure in Houston and Harris County:

  • Day 1-30: You miss your first payment and receive notices from your lender
  • Day 30-60: Late fees accrue, and your lender may start calling
  • Day 90-120: Your loan is officially in default, and foreclosure proceedings may begin
  • Day 120+: Notice of foreclosure sale is posted at the Harris County courthouse
  • First Tuesday of the month: Your home could be sold at the Harris County foreclosure auction

In Texas, foreclosures are typically non-judicial, meaning they don't require court approval. This makes the process much faster than in states like Florida or New York. From the time you receive a Notice of Default to the actual foreclosure sale can be as little as 21 days, though it's usually 2-4 months.

Option 1: Loan Modification or Forbearance

One of the first options to explore is working directly with your lender to modify your loan terms or enter a forbearance agreement.

Loan Modification

A loan modification permanently changes the terms of your mortgage to make payments more affordable. This might include:

  • Reducing your interest rate
  • Extending your loan term (from 30 to 40 years, for example)
  • Adding missed payments to the end of your loan
  • In rare cases, reducing the principal balance

To qualify for a loan modification in Houston, you'll typically need to demonstrate financial hardship and provide extensive documentation including pay stubs, tax returns, bank statements, and a hardship letter explaining your situation.

Forbearance Agreement

Forbearance is a temporary pause or reduction in your mortgage payments, typically lasting 3-12 months. This can be helpful if you're experiencing a short-term setback like recovering from Hurricane Harvey damage, a temporary job loss, or medical issues.

Keep in mind that forbearance doesn't erase what you owe—you'll eventually need to repay those missed payments through a repayment plan, loan modification, or lump sum payment.

Option 2: Refinancing Your Houston Home

If you have equity in your home and your credit hasn't been damaged too severely, refinancing might be an option. Houston's real estate market has seen significant appreciation in neighborhoods like The Heights, Cypress, and Sugar Land over recent years, meaning many homeowners have built substantial equity.

However, refinancing while behind on payments is challenging. Most lenders require:

  • Your mortgage to be current (no late payments in the past 12 months)
  • A credit score of at least 620, preferably higher
  • A debt-to-income ratio below 43%
  • Sufficient equity in your home

If you're only one or two payments behind, you might consider using a personal loan or borrowing from family to bring your mortgage current, then immediately refinancing to lower your monthly payment.

Option 3: Selling Your Houston Home

Sometimes the best option is to sell your home before foreclosure happens. This allows you to leave with your credit intact (or at least minimally damaged) and potentially walk away with cash if you have equity.

Traditional Sale

If you have time and equity, listing your home with a real estate agent might net you the highest price. However, this process typically takes 2-4 months in the Houston market—time you might not have if foreclosure is looming.

Keep in mind you'll also pay:

  • 6% real estate commission (typically split between buyer's and seller's agents)
  • Closing costs (1-3% of sale price)
  • Repairs and staging costs to make your home market-ready

Option 4: Short Sale

If you owe more on your mortgage than your Houston home is worth, a short sale might be your best option. In a short sale, your lender agrees to accept less than the full mortgage balance as payment in full.

Short sales are more common in areas that were hit hard by flooding, such as neighborhoods affected by Hurricane Harvey in 2017. If your home lost value due to flood damage or you purchased at the market peak, you might be underwater on your mortgage.

The short sale process involves:

  1. Getting pre-approval from your lender
  2. Listing your home (usually with an agent experienced in short sales)
  3. Submitting offers to your lender for approval
  4. Waiting for lender approval (can take 2-6 months)
  5. Closing the sale

While a short sale will damage your credit, it's generally less harmful than a foreclosure. You might be able to buy another home in as little as 2-3 years after a short sale, compared to 5-7 years after foreclosure.

Option 5: Deed in Lieu of Foreclosure

A deed in lieu of foreclosure means you voluntarily transfer ownership of your Houston home to your lender in exchange for being released from your mortgage obligation. This option is typically only available if you've already tried to sell your home and been unsuccessful.

Advantages include:

  • Avoids the public foreclosure process
  • Less damage to your credit than foreclosure
  • Faster resolution—you can move on sooner
  • Your lender might offer relocation assistance

However, deed in lieu isn't always available, and you won't receive any proceeds from the sale. Additionally, if you have second liens or other judgments against your property, your primary lender may not accept a deed in lieu.

Option 6: Bankruptcy

Filing for bankruptcy should be considered a last resort, but it can stop foreclosure proceedings immediately through an automatic stay. This gives you breathing room to reorganize your finances.

Chapter 13 Bankruptcy

Chapter 13 allows you to keep your Houston home while repaying your debts over 3-5 years. You'll need to show the bankruptcy court that you have sufficient income to make both your regular mortgage payment and catch up on the arrears through your payment plan.

Chapter 7 Bankruptcy

Chapter 7 discharges most of your unsecured debts but doesn't directly help you keep your home if you're behind on payments. However, by eliminating credit card debt, medical bills, and other obligations, you might free up enough income to resume mortgage payments.

Bankruptcy has serious long-term consequences for your credit and should only be pursued after consulting with a qualified bankruptcy attorney familiar with Texas law.

Houston-Specific Considerations

When you're behind on mortgage payments in Houston, there are some local factors to keep in mind:

Harris County Property Taxes

Houston-area homeowners face some of the highest property taxes in Texas. If you're behind on your mortgage, you're likely also behind on property taxes, since most mortgage payments include escrow for taxes. Remember that in Texas, property tax liens take priority over mortgage liens. Harris County can foreclose on your home for unpaid property taxes separately from your mortgage lender.

Flood Insurance and Hurricane Considerations

If your Houston home is in a flood zone (FEMA zones A or V), you're required to maintain flood insurance if you have a federally backed mortgage. If you've let your flood insurance lapse because you couldn't afford the premiums, you're in violation of your mortgage agreement, which could accelerate foreclosure proceedings.

Additionally, if your home was damaged by flooding from Hurricane Harvey or other storms and you haven't made repairs, this affects your home's value and your options for selling or refinancing.

The Houston Real Estate Market

Houston's real estate market remains relatively strong, with home values in desirable neighborhoods like Bellaire, West University, Memorial, and The Woodlands continuing to appreciate. Even neighborhoods further out like Katy, Pearland, and Conroe have seen steady growth.

This means that many Houston homeowners have equity they can tap into by selling, even if they've fallen behind on payments. The key is acting before foreclosure happens, which can result in selling your home for significantly less than market value at the courthouse auction.

What NOT to Do When You're Behind on Payments

When facing mortgage problems in Houston, avoid these common mistakes:

  • Ignoring the problem: Your lender won't forget about missed payments, and the problem only gets worse with time
  • Avoiding your lender's calls: Communication is key—most lenders prefer to work out a solution rather than foreclose
  • Falling for foreclosure rescue scams: Be wary of companies that ask for upfront fees or promise to stop foreclosure immediately
  • Transferring your deed to someone else: This can constitute mortgage fraud and doesn't actually solve your problem
  • Waiting until the last minute: The earlier you act, the more options you have
